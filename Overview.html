Overview

To learn and explore the life of humans before us, cultural heritage (CH) sites must be preserved. A popular preservation tactic uses digital technologies such as terrestrial laser scanners to scan the CH site and construct applicable 3D models. These models allow preservationists to determine where the site is spoilt for the development of appropriate conservation strategies. The recordings of the scans are stored as point clouds. Point clouds consist of points defined by spatial coordinates (X,Y,Z). However, the point clouds often contain noise and unwanted points from the structure, such as scaffolding, people, animals and vegetation, making an accurate 3D model difficult to obtain. Point cloud cleaning has been explored to discard these unwanted points, but this process is manual, labour-intensive and time-consuming even for a highly skilled individual. Additionally, the structures of CH sites vary, making it tough to formulate a general cleaning model. Therefore, finding an accurate and automatic solution presents a unique challenge.

We propose a framework that tries to simplify and accelerate the point cloud cleaning task by investigating the decomposition of point clouds into semantically meaningful clusters of 3D points through point feature clustering. This materialises though three sub-projects in which partitional, density-based and Hierarchical algorithms are implemented.

Additionally, we explore whether or not clustering can augment the classification process to form groupings that can be classified using binary classification labels of ’keep’ and ’discard’.
